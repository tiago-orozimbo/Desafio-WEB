Physics in everyday life

Talking on the phone, using the computer, heating food in the microwave, riding in the car. Have you ever stopped to think about how much Physics is part of your daily life?

Believe me, it is more present than you imagine!

Firstly, it is worth highlighting that Science plays a fundamental role in human construction, covers the social and philosophical field and systematically and appropriately transfers scientific language and its literacy to society.

The goal? Make communication between men liberating and with fewer restrictions on the individual in their society.

Physics is part of this great range of liberating knowledge and is around us daily. For example, at this very moment, the act of you being able to read this text involves the light that reflects and reaches your eyes, causing numerous physical, chemical and biological processes so that the environment in which you are located is better interpreted and experienced.

At all times you are immersed in the world of Physics. Check out some situations:

Looking at the stars is seeing the past

In order for us to see any object around us, the light produced or reflected by that object must reach our eyes. Light takes a certain amount of time to travel the distance between us and objects, but on a daily basis this time interval is infinitely small compared to the enormous speed of light.

We can only see images of nebulae and stars in the universe because the light from these celestial bodies has traveled through space and reached us. However, when it comes to space, distances cannot be ignored.

Light is affected by gravity

Light travels in a straight line, right? It depends. To affirm this, firstly, it is necessary to admit that the geometry of space is flat, which is not an absolute truth, as explained by Einstein's theory of general relativity.

When light passes through the vicinity of massive stars, such as stars or black holes, its trajectory is altered, since the space itself in these regions is deformed by the large masses of the stars, giving rise to the acceleration of gravity. Therefore, when we observe the stars, we are not looking directly at them, but rather at images of the stars, produced by an effect known as gravitational lensing.

The falling time is independent of the mass

If two objects of different masses are dropped from the same height, they will reach the ground at the same time, if air resistance can be ignored. Our senses tell us that the heavier object should fall faster, but if we drop a bowling ball and a chicken feather at the same height, for example, they will hit the ground together!

The contribution of physics to the development of new knowledge and technologies

Photonics

Photonics develops technologies with applications based on light and its emission, transmission, amplification, etc. As examples we can mention the laser pulses used in industry as a welding tool and in surface treatment, in CD and DVD players, printers and optoelectronic circuits, which are replacing common computer circuits and giving rise to the so-called photonic computing.

Medical Physics

Medical physics deals with the development of knowledge and technologies aimed at treating diseases, diagnoses, surgical equipment, etc. Devices for carrying out detailed ophthalmological surgeries and magnetic resonance equipment are examples of the fantastic contribution of medical physics. Nanotechnology

Nanotechnology allows the individual manipulation of atoms, which can bring contributions beyond what was imagined. With the development of nanotechnology it would be possible, for example, from waste, to reuse atoms of some elements such as carbon, oxygen, nitrogen and hydrogen, reorganize them and create useful elements such as water, oil, etc.

Understanding physics is understanding the world around you!
